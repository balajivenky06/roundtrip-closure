\begin{abstract}
Practitioners deploying multi-stage Small-Language-Model (SLM) pipelines for
software engineering face an unanswered empirical question: does it matter
which SLM sits at which stage? We report the first cross-LLM $\times$
cross-stage evaluation of round-trip closure on the docstring--test--code
triangle, using a pre-registered 20-cell design spanning six open-weight
SLMs under 30~B parameters, three closure paths, and 150 HumanEval and MBPP
functions. Closure is decided under a strict two-signal policy combining an
SE-validated metric (mutation kill rate, reference test pass rate, or
BERTScore F1) with an external judge SLM (DeepSeek-R1). Five findings emerge. First, the pre-registered H1
composition (phi-4 spec + qwen-coder test/code) dominates four of five
mutation operators, matching its per-operator prediction from a companion
mutation-testing study. Second, phi-4 as the test-generation stage produces
filter-failing tests on approximately half of samples across three
independent cells---a cross-stage pathology invisible in single-stage
evaluations. Third, BERTScore F1 is statistically uncorrelated
($r = 0.02$, $p = 0.359$, $n = 1{,}791$) with the judge's semantic rating
on the docstring--docstring path, and the disagreement flips direction
across benchmarks, consistent with a surface-form signal.
Fourth, the three closure paths carry non-redundant information about cell
performance ($F(34, 4865) = 6.21$, $p < 0.001$, partial $\eta^2 = 0.042$).
Fifth, heterogeneity is not free: well-composed hetero cells preserve
$78$--$91\%$ of the strongest mono baseline, while wrong-model-at-wrong-stage
cells collapse. We derive three design guidelines and release an open
replication package including 67 unit tests covering the closure-validity
decision, the test-filter validity gate, and their integration into the
sweep pipeline.
\end{abstract}
